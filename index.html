Buying & Selling in New York

Answers to the most common real estate questions from Queens and Long Island homeowners and buyers.

How much does it cost to sell a house in Queens, NY?

Seller costs in Queens typically include broker commission (negotiable), NY State transfer tax (0.4% of sale price), attorney fees ($1,500–$3,000), and miscellaneous closing costs. I provide a full net proceeds estimate before you decide to list — so you know exactly what you'll walk away with.

How long does it take to sell a home in Queens, New York?

In the current Queens market, well-priced homes typically go under contract in 2–5 weeks. Factors include neighborhood, price point, condition, and time of year. Ozone Park, Richmond Hill, and Jamaica tend to move quickly due to strong buyer demand and limited inventory.

What is my home worth in Queens, NY?

Home values in Queens vary significantly by neighborhood, property type, condition, and lot size. A free Comparative Market Analysis (CMA) from Nitin — based on real OneKey MLS sold data, not automated estimates — will give you an accurate, current valuation of your specific property.

How do I find the best real estate agent in Queens, NY?

Look for an agent with verifiable local sales history, strong online reviews (Google, Zillow, Homes.com), and deep neighborhood knowledge. Nitin Gadura has 7+ years of Queens-specific experience, 57+ five-star reviews, $100M+ in closed volume, and multilingual service across English, Hindi, Punjabi, Gujarati, and Urdu.

Should I sell my house before buying a new one in New York?

In New York's competitive market, selling first gives you maximum negotiating power and eliminates financing contingency risk. Nitin specializes in coordinating simultaneous buy-sell transactions across Queens and Long Island, helping clients time both closings without being caught in between.

What are the best neighborhoods to buy a home in Queens, NY?

Top Queens neighborhoods for buyers depend on your priorities: Ozone Park and Richmond Hill for multi-family investment, Forest Hills and Bayside for single-family prestige, Jamaica for transit access and value, Flushing for high-density upside, and Middle Village for suburban quality at city prices.

How do I sell a multi-family home in Queens, NY?

Multi-family sales in Queens require specific expertise — accurate income analysis, cap rate presentation, tenant disclosure compliance, and access to a buyer pool that includes both investors and owner-occupants. I've sold dozens of 2–4 family homes across Ozone Park, Richmond Hill, Jamaica, and South Ozone Park.

What is the OneKey MLS and why does it matter when selling?

OneKey MLS is the primary Multiple Listing Service for Queens, Nassau, and Suffolk Counties. When I list your home on OneKey MLS, it is automatically syndicated to Zillow, Realtor.com, Homes.com, StreetEasy, Trulia, and 100+ partner sites — giving you maximum exposure from day one.

Do I need to make repairs before selling my Queens home?

Not always. Some sellers get a better return with targeted improvements — fresh paint, landscaping, kitchen hardware updates. Others maximize net proceeds by pricing for condition and selling as-is. I'll walk you through a cost/benefit analysis specific to your home and neighborhood before you spend a dollar.

How do first-time home buyers qualify for a mortgage in New York?

Most first-time buyers in New York need a minimum credit score of 620 (conventional) or 580 (FHA), 3–20% down payment, and a debt-to-income ratio under 43–50%. New York State also offers SONYMA programs with below-market rates and down payment assistance for qualifying first-time buyers.

What is the average home price in Queens, NY?

Queens median home prices range roughly from $550K–$700K for single-family homes, $700K–$1.3M+ for two-family homes, and $400K–$600K for co-ops and condos — though prices vary substantially by neighborhood. Ozone Park and South Ozone Park run lower; Forest Hills, Bayside, and Whitestone run higher.

How do I sell my house fast in Queens, NY?

To sell fast in Queens: price correctly based on real comp data, list on Thursday to capture the weekend buyer surge, use professional photography, and be ready for quick showings. Nitin's listings average under 30 days on market. A free valuation consultation is the fastest first step.

New York buyer closing costs typically total 2–5% of purchase price and include: mortgage recording tax (1.8% on loans under $500K, 1.925% above), title insurance, attorney fees ($1,500–$3,000), lender fees, and property tax/insurance escrow. Co-op buyers also pay flip taxes and move-in deposits. I'll give you a full closing cost estimate before you make any offer.

FHA loans accept scores as low as 580 with 3.5% down. Scores of 500–579 may qualify with 10% down. Some portfolio lenders offer programs for non-traditional credit histories. I can connect you with lenders who specialize in Queens and Long Island buyers with credit challenges.

Two-family (duplex) purchases in Queens are common — many buyers live in one unit and rent the other to offset mortgage costs. You can use FHA financing with as little as 3.5% down on owner-occupied 2–4 family homes. I specialize in multi-family transactions and can walk you through the full process.

New York State real property transfer tax is 0.4% of the sale price. NYC additionally charges 1% on sales $500K and under, and 1.425% on sales above $500K. For properties over $2M, a mansion tax of 1–3.9% applies (paid by buyer). I'll provide a complete seller net proceeds sheet that accounts for all taxes.

Long Island first-time buyers should start with mortgage pre-approval, identify their target neighborhoods by budget and commute needs, and work with an agent who knows the LIRR branch coverage areas. I specialize in helping Queens families make the move to Nassau County communities like Valley Stream, Elmont, Hempstead, and Hicksville.

Key seller documents include: deed, property survey, title insurance policy, any prior inspection reports, certificate of occupancy, mortgage payoff statements, and utility/tax records. Your attorney handles most document preparation; I'll connect you with experienced Queens and Long Island real estate attorneys from day one.

Queens remains a seller-favorable market in most neighborhoods due to historically low inventory relative to buyer demand. Spring and early fall are peak selling seasons. However, well-priced, well-presented homes sell in every season. I can pull current market data for your specific neighborhood and give you a precise read on timing.

How to sell a house in Queens, New York — Step by Step

Selling a home in Queens involves: (1) getting a comparative market analysis, (2) selecting a listing price, (3) preparing the home for photos and showings, (4) listing on OneKey MLS and all major portals, (5) reviewing and negotiating offers, (6) managing inspection and attorney review, and (7) closing. I guide you through every step with zero guesswork.

How to buy a home in Queens, NY — A Complete Buyer's Guide

Buying a home in Queens starts with mortgage pre-approval, followed by neighborhood selection based on commute, schools, and budget. Queens offers co-ops, condos, attached homes, and detached single- and multi-family properties. The market is competitive — having an agent with deep local knowledge and strong relationships is the single biggest advantage a buyer can have.

Queens, NY multi-family home investing — what you need to know

Queens is one of the strongest multi-family investment markets in the US. Two- and three-family homes in Ozone Park, Richmond Hill, Jamaica, and South Jamaica consistently deliver cap rates of 4–6%. Key metrics: gross rent multiplier, net operating income, and cash-on-cash return. I've helped dozens of investors analyze and acquire income-producing properties across Queens and Nassau County.

What is a CMA (Comparative Market Analysis) and how does it work?

A CMA is a detailed analysis of recently sold, currently active, and expired listings similar to your property. It accounts for square footage, lot size, condition, bedroom/bathroom count, garage, location within the neighborhood, and market timing. Unlike Zillow's Zestimate — which uses automated algorithms — a Nitin Gadura CMA is built from real OneKey MLS data with local interpretation.

How to sell a two-family home in Queens — maximizing your sale price

Two-family homes in Queens are marketed to two distinct buyer types: owner-occupants (who live in one unit and rent the other) and pure investors (who analyze cap rates and NOI). To maximize your sale price, your listing needs to speak to both audiences simultaneously — with accurate rent rolls, expense schedules, and a clear investment story alongside the residential appeal. I've perfected this dual-market approach across hundreds of Queens multi-family transactions.

How long does a real estate closing take in New York?

New York real estate closings typically take 60–90 days from accepted offer to closing for financed transactions; all-cash closings can close in 30–45 days. The attorney review period, co-op board approval (if applicable), mortgage commitment, and title search are the primary timeline drivers. I maintain relationships with attorneys and lenders who keep transactions moving efficiently.

What is the difference between a real estate agent and a real estate broker in New York?

In New York, a real estate salesperson (agent) is licensed to represent buyers and sellers under the supervision of a licensed broker. What neighborhoods in Queens have the highest home values?

The highest home values in Queens are typically found in: Whitestone and Douglaston-Little Neck (premium detached homes, LIRR access), Forest Hills Gardens (landmark planned community), Bayside and Bay Terrace (suburban character, strong schools), and Howard Beach (waterfront properties).
